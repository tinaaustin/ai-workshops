# AI Educator Starter Kit

A short public starter kit for educators beginning to make sense of AI in teaching and assessment.

This resource is intentionally brief. The full UnBlooms framework, workbook exercises, rubrics, classroom activities, and implementation guidance are available in the [UnBlooms book](https://www.amazon.com/dp/B0GKZXXW4G?ref=cm_sw_r_ffobk_cp_ud_dp_M60YZKM1Q9ANH60FGSKJ&ref_=cm_sw_r_ffobk_cp_ud_dp_M60YZKM1Q9ANH60FGSKJ&social_share=cm_sw_r_ffobk_cp_ud_dp_M60YZKM1Q9ANH60FGSKJ&bestFormat=true).

## 1. Start With The Learning Goal

Before deciding whether AI should be allowed, limited, or prohibited, ask:

- What thinking should students practice?
- What part of the task should remain human?
- What would count as evidence of learning?
- Could AI produce the product without the student doing the thinking?

## 2. Name The AI Boundary

Use clear assignment language. Avoid vague phrases like "use AI responsibly" unless students know what that means in context.

Common boundaries:

- **No AI:** The task depends on unaided human practice.
- **AI for support:** AI may help with brainstorming, organization, or revision.
- **AI for critique:** Students evaluate AI output rather than submit it as their own.
- **AI with documentation:** Students may use AI, but must explain how and why.

## 3. Require Process Evidence

When AI can produce polished work, educators need ways to see the learning process.

Possible process evidence:

- initial notes or outline
- draft history
- prompt log
- source checks
- revision notes
- short reflection
- explanation of accepted and rejected AI suggestions

## 4. Teach Verification

Students should learn to treat AI output as a claim to investigate.

Useful verification questions:

- Is this accurate?
- What evidence supports it?
- What is missing?
- Whose perspective is absent?
- Does this match the standards of the discipline?
- What would an expert question?

## 5. Keep Human Judgment Visible

AI can assist with language, structure, examples, and revision. It cannot replace disciplinary judgment, ethical reasoning, lived context, or responsibility for final work.
