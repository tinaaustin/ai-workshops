# AI Education Glossary

A brief, educator-friendly glossary for common terms in AI, teaching, learning, and assessment.

This is a public orientation resource. The full UnBlooms framework, workbook exercises, rubrics, classroom activities, and implementation materials are available in the UnBlooms book.

## Glossary

**AI literacy**  
The ability to understand what AI tools can do, where they fail, how to evaluate their output, and when their use is appropriate.

**Generative AI**  
AI that creates new content, such as text, images, code, summaries, examples, or study materials.

**Large language model (LLM)**  
An AI model trained on large amounts of text that predicts and generates language.

**Prompt**  
The instruction, question, or context a user gives an AI tool.

**AI output**  
The text, image, code, explanation, summary, or other response produced by an AI tool.

**Hallucination**  
An AI-generated claim that sounds plausible but is false, unsupported, fabricated, or misleading.

**Bias**  
A pattern in AI output that reflects unfair assumptions, missing perspectives, skewed data, or stereotypes.

**Verification**  
Checking AI output against reliable sources, evidence, disciplinary standards, calculations, or expert judgment.

**Metacognition**  
Thinking about one's own thinking. In AI-rich learning, this includes noticing when AI is helping, when it is replacing thought, and how one's understanding changes.

**Cognitive offloading**  
Using a tool to reduce mental effort. In education, this becomes a concern when the tool does the thinking students are meant to practice.

**Process documentation**  
Evidence of how a student approached a task, including decisions, drafts, prompts, revisions, checks, and reflections.

**Reasoning trail**  
A record of the steps, judgments, and changes behind a final product.

**AI-constrained assessment**  
An assessment that limits or excludes AI use because the learning goal depends on unaided human practice, live performance, personal voice, or direct demonstration.

**AI-integrated assessment**  
An assessment that allows or requires AI use while making students responsible for critique, verification, revision, transparency, and reflection.

**Transparency statement**  
A short student explanation of whether and how AI was used in an assignment.

**Academic integrity**  
The ethical practice of doing and representing academic work honestly. In AI-rich classrooms, integrity requires clear expectations about acceptable use.

**Human-in-the-loop**  
A process where a person remains responsible for reviewing, deciding, correcting, and contextualizing AI output.

**Automation bias**  
The tendency to trust automated output because it appears confident, polished, or technical.

**UnBlooms**  
A framework for rethinking teaching, assessment, and critical thinking in the age of AI, with attention to visible reasoning, metacognition, and human judgment.

For the complete framework and classroom-ready materials, see the UnBlooms book.
